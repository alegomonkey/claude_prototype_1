# Skill: faculty-translation

## Trigger
Invoked after `learning-plan` (to prepare the advisor for a faculty conversation) or directly from `session-start` when the advisor is preparing for a faculty meeting without needing to revisit student goals.

## Purpose
Translate student goals and alternative assignment proposals into faculty-friendly language. The core argument is always: **same learning objectives, different evidence of competency**. Surface institutional constraints before the conversation — not after. Help the advisor walk into the faculty meeting confident and prepared, not caught off guard.

## What to do

### Step 1 — Identify the audience
Ask who the advisor is speaking with:
> "Who are you bringing this to — the course instructor, a department chair, a curriculum committee, or someone else?"

The language and framing shift depending on audience:
- **Instructor:** Focus on pedagogical value, feasibility, and student motivation
- **Department chair:** Focus on program outcomes, accreditation alignment, institutional precedent
- **Curriculum committee:** Focus on systemic patterns and policy implications

### Step 2 — Run the constraint checklist
Before drafting any talking points, explicitly work through:

> Let me check a few things before we frame this:
> 1. **Accreditation/licensure:** Does this course or program have external accreditation requirements that constrain assessment format? (Engineering, nursing, teacher certification, and business programs often do.)
> 2. **Instructor discretion:** Does the instructor have documented ability to modify assessment formats, or does that require approval?
> 3. **Standardization:** Is this course taught the same way across multiple sections? If so, the alternative may need to be proposed at the course level, not just for one student.
> 4. **Precedent:** Has a similar alternative been approved before in this program? Knowing this strengthens the case.
> 5. **Degree requirements:** Does the student's degree audit require a specific competency demonstration from this course? If so, the alternative must explicitly cover it.

**Flag any unresolved constraints as risks** — do not proceed as if they don't exist.

### Step 3 — Draft the talking points
For each alternative assignment, produce a faculty-facing framing:

**Format:**
> **[Course name] — Alternative for [student name]**
> - Standard assignment: [what the current assignment asks for]
> - Proposed alternative: [concrete alternative]
> - Same objectives met: [list the specific learning objectives the alternative satisfies]
> - Additional value: [any skills or outcomes the alternative adds that the standard assignment doesn't]
> - What we need from you: [specific ask — approval, flexibility, a conversation]

### Step 4 — Anticipate objections
For each proposal, name the most likely faculty objection and prepare a response:

> "The most common concern here is [objection]. If that comes up, here's how to respond: [response]."

Common objections:
- "This isn't fair to other students" → Response: frame as documented accommodation based on advising goals, not special treatment
- "I can't grade this the same way" → Response: propose a shared rubric mapped to the standard learning objectives
- "The department won't approve exceptions" → Response: check Step 2 constraints first; if this is a real constraint, say so before the meeting

### Step 5 — Coach the framing
Remind the advisor of the core argument they should lead with:
> "The through-line for this conversation is: [student name]'s alternative meets all the same learning objectives as the standard assignment. We're not asking to reduce rigor — we're proposing a different form of evidence."

Offer to role-play the conversation if the advisor wants practice.

## What NOT to do
- Do not frame alternatives as "easier" or "more motivating" — lead with learning objectives
- Do not present any alternative as approved or viable before the constraint checklist is complete
- Do not promise the faculty will say yes — the advisor's job is to make the best case, not to guarantee an outcome
- Do not skip the objection-anticipation step — surprises in the faculty meeting undermine the advisor's credibility

## Tone
Strategic and precise. This skill makes the advisor sound like they've done their homework — because they have. Confident but not presumptuous about what faculty will accept.
