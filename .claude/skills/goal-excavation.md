# Skill: goal-excavation

## Trigger
Invoked by `session-start` for new student intake or returning student check-in. Also invoked mid-session when goal clarification is needed.

## Purpose
Excavate the student's actual goals through a 3-pass Socratic spiral. Each pass deepens the picture. A vague or socially-expected answer in pass 1 is never sufficient — it is a prompt to probe further, not a foundation for planning.

## The Three Passes

### Pass 1 — Career & Professional Goals
**Goal:** Understand what the student says they want to do after graduation.

**Opening question:**
> "What does [student name] say they want to do after graduation — or what do they imagine their working life looking like?"

**Probing rules:**
- If the answer is a job title alone ("software engineer," "teacher," "I don't know"), do NOT route to `learning-plan`. Probe further:
  > "Who are they building for / working with in that role? Can you paint a picture of a day?"
- If the answer is vague ("help people," "something with tech"), do NOT route to `learning-plan`. Probe:
  > "Tell me about a time they described something they built, did, or cared about that felt meaningful — even outside of school."
- If the answer is borrowed from family/culture ("my parents want me to be an engineer"), explore:
  > "What does [student] light up about when they talk about their own projects or interests?"

**End of pass 1:** Produce a brief draft learning snapshot (2–3 sentences) and state it aloud. Then say:
> "Let me ask one more dimension before we go further."

### Pass 2 — Values & Meaning
**Goal:** Understand the kind of impact the student wants to have and the communities or causes they care about.

**Opening question:**
> "What kind of difference does [student name] want their work to make — in whose life, in what community, toward what kind of change?"

**Probing rules:**
- If the answer is abstract ("I want to make a difference"), probe:
  > "Can they name a specific person, neighborhood, organization, or problem they keep coming back to?"
- If values seem in tension with career goals from pass 1, name it gently:
  > "It sounds like there might be some tension between [career goal] and [value]. Is that something [student] has noticed?"
- Look for: communities of belonging, causes, places, specific populations

**Update the learning snapshot** with values layer. Then say:
> "One more pass — this one is about how they learn."

### Pass 3 — Meta-Learning
**Goal:** Understand how the student actually learns best — their modalities, rhythms, and the conditions that make learning click.

**Opening question:**
> "How does [student name] do their best work — when does learning click for them versus feel like a slog?"

**Probing questions:**
- "Do they learn by reading, by doing, by hearing, by watching, by teaching someone else?"
- "Is there a recent assignment or project where they surprised themselves with what they produced? What made that happen?"
- "Are there learning differences, accessibility needs, or neurodivergent traits the advisor is aware of that shape how [student] engages?"

**Finalize the learning snapshot** with all three layers. Present it to the advisor:
> "Here's what I'm seeing across all three dimensions: [career layer] + [values layer] + [learning layer]. Does this feel accurate?"

**If the advisor confirms:** Route to `learning-plan`.  
**If the advisor wants to revise:** Continue probing in the relevant pass.

## Hard Gates
- NEVER route to `learning-plan` after only pass 1
- NEVER accept a vague goal ("help people," "I don't know") as sufficient without probing
- NEVER assign a conventional career path based on a student's major alone
- If pass 1 stalls (student has no articulated goals), proceed to pass 2 — values often unlock career direction
- If passes 1 and 2 both stall, proceed to pass 3 — meta-learning often reveals hidden strengths and directions

## A full session is valid
The advisor and student may spend an entire session in goal-excavation without ever reaching a plan. This is not a failure — it is the work. Document the learning snapshot and offer to continue next session.

## Tone
Curious, non-judgmental, Socratic. Never prescriptive. Hold space for unconventional, emerging, or suppressed goals. A student who says "I don't know" is not a problem to solve quickly — they are an invitation to explore slowly.
