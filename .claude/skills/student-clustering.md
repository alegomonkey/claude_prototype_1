# Skill: student-clustering

## Trigger
Invoked by `session-start` when the advisor has 3 or more student profiles and wants to find patterns, form learning groups, or design shared experiences.

## Purpose
Identify students with meaningfully overlapping goals, values, or learning styles. Name the shared theme. Propose a small learning group (2–4 students) with a clear rationale. Give the advisor something concrete to bring to faculty.

## What to do

### Step 1 — Gather profiles
Ask the advisor to share (or confirm) the students they want to cluster:
> "Which students are we looking across? I'll need their goal snapshots — either from prior sessions or a quick summary now."

If profiles are incomplete, offer to run `goal-excavation` on missing students before clustering.

### Step 2 — Identify overlaps
Look across student profiles on all three dimensions:
- **Career:** Are any students aiming at the same industry, sector, community, or role type?
- **Values:** Do any share a cause, community, or definition of meaningful work?
- **Meta-learning:** Do any share a learning modality (project-based, conversation-based, auditory, applied)?

Overlaps on 2 of 3 dimensions = strong cluster candidate.  
Overlap on 1 dimension = weak — note it but don't force a group.

### Step 3 — Name the shared theme
Give the cluster a short descriptive name that captures what the students share:
> "Devon, Marcus, and Sasha all want practitioner-connected work in educational equity — I'd call this the 'Community Equity Research' cluster."

The name should mean something to the students, not just the advisor.

### Step 4 — Propose the group structure
Recommend group size (2–4), explain why these students belong together, and suggest one shared learning experience:
- A joint project replacing individual assignments
- A reading group or peer seminar
- A shared external partnership or community client

Frame the proposal around what the students gain from each other — not just administrative convenience.

### Step 5 — Flag mismatches
If a student doesn't cleanly fit a cluster, say so:
> "Priyanka shares Devon's values but her learning style (applied, project-based) is different from Marcus and Sasha. She might work better as a peer consultant to the group rather than a full member."

## What NOT to do
- Do not force students into a cluster because they share a major or course
- Do not create groups larger than 4 — small groups learn together better
- Do not cluster students whose goals conflict in ways that would create friction without benefit
- Do not skip the "name the theme" step — unnamed groups don't cohere

## Route after clustering
Always route to `learning-plan` after a cluster is formed.

## Tone
Analytical and precise, but always focused on what's good for the students. Synthesizer, not sorter.
