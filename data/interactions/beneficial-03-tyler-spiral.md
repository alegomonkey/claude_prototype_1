# Beneficial Interaction 03 — Tyler: The Spiral

**Personas involved:** Advisor, Tyler  
**Skills used:** `session-start` → `goal-excavation` (all 3 passes, pass 1 intentionally stalls) → `learning-plan`  
**Outcome:** Beneficial — agent refuses to produce a plan from a stalled pass 1; uses values and meta-learning passes to surface genuine strengths; produces a plan that serves Tyler's actual learning needs without forcing a premature career direction

---

## Session

**Advisor:** I want to work on Tyler. He's an undeclared sophomore. Honestly I'm not sure what to do with him — he seems pretty checked out.

**Agent (session-start):**
> Let's do a full intake on Tyler. I'll start with career goals but I want to flag upfront: when a student seems "checked out," it often means the standard questions don't fit them. Pass 1 may stall, and that's okay — passes 2 and 3 are where we'll learn something real. Ready?

**Advisor:** Ready.

---

### Pass 1 — Career Goals (stalls)

**Agent (goal-excavation):**
> What does Tyler say he wants to do after graduation — or what does he imagine his working life looking like?

**Advisor:** He says he doesn't know. I asked him what he's interested in and he said "music, I guess."

**Agent:**
> "I don't know" plus "music, I guess" — that's a pass 1 stall, which is normal. Let me probe before we move on.
>
> Has Tyler ever described something he built or made — not for class, just because he wanted to?

**Advisor:** Actually yes. He's built two synthesizers. DIY, from circuit diagrams he found online. He was telling me about one of them and I didn't really follow what he was saying.

**Agent:**
> That's significant. Building synthesizers from circuit diagrams is not a casual hobby — it requires reading schematics, understanding signal flow, troubleshooting analog circuits. That's serious applied systems thinking.
>
> *Draft snapshot (pass 1):* Career direction unknown. No borrowed goal, no suppressed goal visible yet. Genuine uncertainty. Real-world evidence of capability: two DIY synthesizer builds. Underlying skill set: systems thinking, signal flow, analog electronics, pattern recognition from circuit diagrams. "Music" may be the surface; the interest may run deeper.
>
> Not enough to build from yet. Let's go to pass 2.

---

### Pass 2 — Values

**Agent:**
> When Tyler is working on one of his builds — or when he talks about them — what does he describe? What does he care about in that work?

**Advisor:** He said once that he likes "when the pieces fit together and you can hear it working." He talks about the oscillator and the filter like they're having a conversation.

**Agent:**
> "When the pieces fit together and you can hear it working." That's a values statement about how he understands the world — through systems, through feedback, through the tangible result of things working in relationship. He experiences learning physically and aurally, not abstractly.
>
> Does Tyler talk about making music *for* anyone, or is it more about the building itself?

**Advisor:** More the building. He plays in a band but when he talks about music he mostly talks about the equipment.

**Agent:**
> So the interest is systems, not performance. The music is the domain; the real engagement is with how things work together and what happens when they do.
>
> *Updated snapshot (pass 2):* Career — still unknown, but the shape is clearer: Tyler is drawn to systems and their behavior, particularly in domains he can experience directly. Values — understanding how things connect and interact; the satisfaction of a working system; building something with audible, tangible results. He's not performing for an audience; he's engineering for the experience of making something work.

---

### Pass 3 — Meta-Learning

**Agent:**
> How does Tyler actually learn? When does something click for him versus feel like a wall?

**Advisor:** He aces labs but skips most lectures. He submitted a lab report once that showed he understood the underlying concepts better than most of the class — but he couldn't tell me which chapter it came from. He just figured it out by doing.

**Agent:**
> That's a clear pattern: kinesthetic and auditory learner who needs to encounter a concept through practice before the theory makes sense. The textbook-first model is backwards for him.
>
> I also want to raise something gently: the combination of "aces labs, skips lectures, learns by doing, struggles to engage with abstract instruction" is a profile that sometimes goes with an undiagnosed learning difference — possibly ADHD, dyslexia, or an auditory processing style that standard lectures don't support. Has Tyler ever been referred to accessibility services?

**Advisor:** No. I hadn't thought about that.

**Agent:**
> It might be worth a conversation — not a diagnosis, just an exploration. Accessibility services can sometimes open up alternative instruction formats that make a significant difference.
>
> *Final snapshot:*
> - **Career:** Genuinely unknown — and that's okay. The shape of his interest is systems thinking in applied, sensory domains. Sound engineering, audio electronics, human-computer interaction, embedded systems are all possibilities. He doesn't need a career title right now; he needs to see that his mind works in a way that has real applications.
> - **Values:** Understanding how systems work together; the satisfaction of a functioning build; tangible, audible results.
> - **Meta-learning:** Kinesthetic and auditory; encounters concepts through practice first; labs click, lectures don't. Possible accessibility needs worth exploring.
>
> Does this feel accurate?

**Advisor:** Yes. I feel like I understand him now in a way I didn't an hour ago.

### Learning Plan

**Agent (learning-plan):**
> Here's what I'd propose for Tyler's current semester. Note: I'm not going to force a career direction into this plan. The goal right now is to make his coursework more engaging and surface his learning style — a career picture can emerge over time.

---

> **Introduction to Computing**
> - Standard objective: Logic, algorithms, data representation, basic programming
> - Goal connection: Tyler already understands analog circuit logic from his synthesizer work — he just hasn't connected it to formal computing concepts
> - Alternative assignment idea: Propose audio synthesis as the domain for a logic circuits project — designing a simple digital oscillator or signal processor using the logic gates covered in class. The formal content is identical; the domain is one Tyler already thinks in.

---

> **First-Year Seminar** (assuming reflective/writing focus)
> - Standard objective: Goal-setting, academic planning, reflective writing
> - Goal connection: Tyler's actual learning style is the most important thing he could understand about himself right now
> - Alternative assignment idea: Replace the standard "academic goal-setting" essay with a "How I Learn" reflection — describing the synthesizer builds, what the experience taught him, and how he learns best. Frame it as evidence-based self-knowledge rather than career planning. Same reflective writing objectives; more authentic material.

---

> **Calculus I**
> - Standard objective: Limits, derivatives, integration
> - Note: Less flexibility here, and Tyler may genuinely need tutoring support. However — the mathematics of signal processing (Fourier transforms, frequency analysis) are directly downstream of calculus. Consider mentioning this connection to Tyler to give him a reason to care about what he's currently learning.

---

> **Two additional recommendations (not assignment alternatives):**
> 1. Refer Tyler to accessibility services for a learning styles conversation — not framed as "something might be wrong" but as "we want to understand how you learn best so we can support you."
> 2. Connect Tyler with any audio tech, electronics, or maker-space resources on campus. He may not know they exist, and one meaningful connection to a community can shift his engagement entirely.
