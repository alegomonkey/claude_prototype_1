# Beneficial Interaction 01 — Amara: Community Tech

**Personas involved:** Advisor (unnamed), Amara  
**Skills used:** `session-start` → `goal-excavation` (all 3 passes) → `learning-plan` → `faculty-translation`  
**Outcome:** Beneficial — agent surfaces suppressed goals across multiple passes; produces an institutionally-grounded plan; flags a real constraint before the faculty meeting

---

## Session

**Advisor:** I have a new student I'd like to start with. Her name is Amara — sophomore, CS major, hasn't declared a concentration yet. She's been quiet in class but does exceptional work on concrete projects.

**Agent (session-start):**
> Welcome. It sounds like we're doing a first intake for Amara. I'll start by excavating her goals — we'll do at least two passes before building anything. Ready?

**Advisor:** Yes, let's go.

---

### Pass 1 — Career Goals

**Agent (goal-excavation):**
> What does Amara say she wants to do after graduation — or what does she imagine her working life looking like?

**Advisor:** She says she wants to be a software engineer. At a big company, probably. That's what her parents expect.

**Agent:**
> That's a starting point. Let me probe a little. When you picture Amara in that role — who is she building for? What does a day actually look like?

**Advisor:** Honestly, I'm not sure. She hasn't really described it.

**Agent:**
> That gap is worth exploring. Has she ever described a project — school or personal — where she really came alive? Something she built or worked on that felt meaningful to her specifically?

**Advisor:** Actually yes — she mentioned her aunt's food pantry twice. Something about a manual inventory system that wastes food. I didn't follow up on it.

**Agent:**
> That's the thread. The food pantry is probably where her real career picture lives, not the generic software engineer answer. Let me note a draft snapshot and move to pass 2.
>
> *Draft snapshot (pass 1):* Amara names "software engineer" as her goal, but this appears to be a borrowed goal — the expected answer for a first-gen CS student from her family context. Possible real direction: community-facing technology, food systems or food security, building for people she knows.

---

### Pass 2 — Values

**Agent:**
> What kind of impact does Amara want her work to have — in whose life, in what community, toward what kind of change?

**Advisor:** She's talked about her neighborhood a few times. Her family is still there, she wants to stay connected. She mentioned food security once as something she cares about but said "that's not a tech thing."

**Agent:**
> "That's not a tech thing" is a belief worth naming back to her — because it's not true, and it may be the belief that's suppressing her real direction. Food justice, community tech, civic infrastructure — these are all tech things.
>
> *Updated snapshot (pass 2):* Career — community-facing technology, likely food systems or food security. Values — staying connected to her neighborhood and family; food justice; building for people she knows and who depend on the work. She may not yet have language for this as a career path.

---

### Pass 3 — Meta-Learning

**Agent:**
> How does Amara do her best work — when does learning click for her versus feel like a slog?

**Advisor:** You can see it in her projects. When the problem is concrete and she knows someone who will actually use what she builds, the work is exceptional. Abstract problem sets — she does them but they're clearly just getting it done.

**Agent:**
> That's a clear learning style: project-based, end-user oriented, intrinsically motivated by real-world application. She needs a real human on the other end of her code.
>
> *Final snapshot:*
> - **Career:** Community-facing technology, likely food systems. "Software engineer" is a borrowed goal.
> - **Values:** Food justice, staying connected to her neighborhood, building for people she knows.
> - **Meta-learning:** Learns by doing for a real person; abstract problems demotivate; concrete community-facing projects bring out her best work.
>
> Does this feel accurate?

**Advisor:** Yes — that's Amara exactly. I've never heard it described that clearly.

---

### Learning Plan

**Agent (learning-plan):**
> Here's a plan across Amara's current courses. I'll flag the institutional constraint check at the end.

---

> **Data Structures**
> - Standard objective: Implement and analyze fundamental data structures (lists, trees, graphs, hash maps)
> - Goal connection: Same structures apply directly to food distribution routing, inventory management, and neighborhood resource mapping
> - Alternative assignment idea: Replace the generic linked list problem set with a food donation routing problem — modeling a network of pantries and donors using the same data structures. Same algorithmic complexity analysis required.

---

> **Systems Programming**
> - Standard objective: Understand OS-level concepts — memory management, concurrency, file I/O
> - Goal connection: Any real inventory or logistics system Amara builds will require exactly these skills
> - Alternative assignment idea: The standard midterm exam assesses OS concepts individually. *See constraint flag below.* For the final project, propose: build a lightweight inventory tracking system for the food pantry — real file I/O, concurrent access from multiple volunteers, error handling under real conditions.

---

> **Technical Writing**
> - Standard objective: Write clearly for technical and non-technical audiences; document systems
> - Goal connection: Direct fit — Amara's end users are pantry staff, not engineers
> - Alternative assignment idea: Replace the standard technical documentation assignment with user documentation for the pantry system — a volunteer quick-start guide, an error FAQ, and a one-page summary for the pantry director. Same writing objectives; authentic audience.

---

> **Constraint check before we proceed:**
>
> I want to flag one risk. Systems Programming at many CS programs is tied to ABET accreditation requirements — the midterm exam may need to assess OS concepts in a standardized format that the instructor can't modify without department approval, or at all. Before Amara builds any expectation around changing that exam, please confirm:
> 1. Does this course fall under ABET accreditation for the CS program?
> 2. Does the instructor have discretion to substitute the midterm format, or is that set at the department level?
>
> The final project alternative is more likely to be feasible than the midterm substitution.

**Advisor:** Good catch. I'll check on ABET before we mention the midterm to Amara.

**Agent:**
> Smart. Let's only bring to Amara what we're confident is actually available. Should I draft the faculty translation for what we do bring?

**Advisor:** Yes please.

---

### Faculty Translation

**Agent (faculty-translation):**
> Here's how to frame the plan for Amara's instructors.

---

> **Technical Writing — Talking points for the instructor**
>
> Standard assignment: Technical documentation for a hypothetical system  
> Proposed alternative: User documentation for a real food pantry inventory system — quick-start guide for volunteers, error FAQ, and a director summary  
> Same objectives met: Audience analysis, document structure, technical accuracy, writing for non-technical readers  
> Additional value: Authentic audience and real-use context; student can gather feedback from actual users  
> What we need: Instructor approval to substitute the real-world documentation for the hypothetical one  
>
> Likely objection: "How do I grade something I can't verify is real?" → Response: The rubric stays the same — audience awareness, clarity, structure, accuracy. The subject matter changes, not the evaluation criteria.

---

> **Data Structures — Talking points for the instructor**
>
> Standard assignment: Linked list and graph implementation problem sets  
> Proposed alternative: Same implementations applied to a food donation routing problem  
> Same objectives met: Identical data structure implementations, identical complexity analysis requirements  
> Additional value: Contextualizes abstract structures in a real-world domain; no grading change required  
> What we need: Instructor flexibility to accept a domain-specific problem framing  
>
> Likely objection: "Other students don't get this option." → Response: Amara is completing the identical technical requirements. The domain context is the accommodation, not a reduction in rigor.

---

> **Through-line for both conversations:**
> "Amara's alternatives meet all the same learning objectives as the standard assignments. We're not asking to reduce rigor — we're proposing that she demonstrate competency through problems connected to her actual career direction. Same destination, different road."

---

**Advisor:** This is exactly what I needed. I feel ready for both conversations.

**Agent:**
> One last thing: before you bring any of this to Amara, confirm the ABET/midterm question with the Systems Programming instructor first. Once that's resolved, the plan has no unverified assumptions.
