# Beneficial Interaction 02 — Devon: Student Clustering

**Personas involved:** Advisor, Devon (M.Ed., Education Policy), Marcus (22, M.Ed., teacher retention policy), Sasha (26, M.Ed., multilingual learner policy)  
**Skills used:** `session-start` → `student-clustering` → `learning-plan` (group) → `faculty-translation`  
**Outcome:** Beneficial — agent identifies a meaningful shared theme across three students with overlapping values; proposes a group alternative that strengthens all three individually

---

## Session

**Advisor:** I have three M.Ed. students I want to look at together. Devon, Marcus, and Sasha. They're all in the same Research Methods cohort, and I think there might be something there.

**Agent (session-start):**
> Sounds like a clustering session. Do you have goal snapshots for all three, or should we build them first?

**Advisor:** Devon's snapshot is complete from our last session. Marcus and Sasha I have rough notes on but no full snapshot.

**Agent:**
> Let me work with what you have and flag where we need more depth. Walk me through Marcus and Sasha quickly.

**Advisor:** Marcus is 22, wants to work on teacher retention policy — specifically why experienced teachers leave high-need schools. He's frustrated that his coursework focuses on policy design but not implementation. Sasha is 26, focused on multilingual learner policy — she worked with ELL students before grad school and wants to go back to that community. She says the Research Methods course feels "made for people who've never been in a classroom."

---

### Clustering

**Agent (student-clustering):**
> Here's what I'm seeing across Devon, Marcus, and Sasha:
>
> **Career overlap:** All three are oriented toward equity work in K-12 education, specifically in communities that are underserved or underrepresented in standard policy discourse.
>
> **Values overlap:** All three describe frustration with theory-for-theory's-sake. They want their work to land with practitioners — teachers, families, district staff — not just in academic journals. Devon said "I want to work with people, not about them." Marcus is annoyed by implementation gaps. Sasha feels the course is designed for people who haven't been in the communities they're studying.
>
> **Meta-learning overlap:** All three seem to learn better through practitioner contact and applied case work than through theoretical readings.
>
> **Cluster name:** "Community Equity Research" — three students who want practitioner-connected equity work in K-12, frustrated by the academic abstraction of their current program.
>
> **Group size:** 3 — ideal. Tight enough to coordinate, diverse enough in sub-focus (retention, multilingual, consulting) to learn from each other.
>
> Does this cluster feel accurate?

**Advisor:** Yes, exactly. I didn't have that language for it but that's right.

---

### Group Learning Plan

**Agent (learning-plan):**
> Here's a shared learning experience I'd propose for Research Methods — the course they're all in together.

---

> **Research Methods — Group Alternative**
>
> Standard assignment: Individual policy memo — literature review + policy recommendation on an assigned topic  
> Goal connection: All three want to build skills in practitioner-connected research; the standard memo produces academic writing for an academic audience
>
> Proposed group alternative: A **joint practitioner-consultation project** in three parts:
> 1. Each student interviews 1–2 teachers, district staff, or families in their focus area (retention, multilingual, consulting) — 3–6 practitioner voices total
> 2. The group synthesizes findings across all three focus areas, identifying shared themes and divergences
> 3. Final output: a practitioner brief (not a policy memo) + a 15-minute presentation to the cohort, with one of Devon's district contacts invited as a respondent
>
> Same research methods objectives met: Literature engagement (each student situates their interviews in existing research), systematic data collection, synthesis and argumentation, written communication
>
> Additional skills: Primary data collection, practitioner communication, cross-topic synthesis, presenting to a mixed academic/practitioner audience
>
> What we need: Research Methods instructor approval to substitute the group project for the individual memo

---

> **Constraint check:**
> 1. Is the Research Methods final a program requirement that must be completed individually? Some graduate programs require individual assessment for a methods course.
> 2. Does the instructor have flexibility on the assignment format, or is it set at the program level?
> 3. Is there a grading equity concern if three students collaborate on a final while the rest of the cohort submits individually? We'll need to address this with the instructor.

**Advisor:** I don't think it's required to be individual, but I'll confirm. The instructor is generally open — she's used real-world case work before. The equity concern is real though.

**Agent:**
> Good. The equity response is straightforward: each student's individual contribution to the project is documented and graded separately. The synthesis section is joint; the interview section is individual. The rubric maps to individual competency, not just group output.

---

### Faculty Translation

**Agent (faculty-translation):**
> Here's how to frame this for the Research Methods instructor.

---

> **Research Methods — Group Alternative Talking Points**
>
> Standard assignment: Individual policy memo  
> Proposed alternative: Joint practitioner-consultation project — individual interviews, group synthesis, shared brief and presentation  
> Same objectives met: Literature synthesis, systematic data collection, argumentation, written and oral communication  
> Additional value: Adds primary data collection and practitioner communication — skills the standard memo does not develop  
> What we need: Instructor approval; clarity on individual vs. group grading structure  
>
> Likely objection: "This isn't fair to students who submitted individual memos." → Response: The group project requires equivalent individual work (each student conducts their own interviews and situates them in literature). The synthesis is collaborative, but the core methods competencies are demonstrated individually. We can make that explicit in the rubric.
>
> Likely objection: "How do I assess individual quality in a group product?" → Response: Propose a split rubric — individual interview memo (50%) + group brief and presentation (50%). Or ask each student to submit a brief individual reflection on what they contributed and what they learned.

---

> **Through-line:**
> "Devon, Marcus, and Sasha are each pursuing equity work in K-12 communities. The research methods skills this course teaches are exactly what they need — but the standard memo format doesn't let them develop primary research skills or practitioner communication, which are central to the careers they're building. This alternative develops the same competencies with a more authentic application."

---

Frame it as: "This project has the potential to produce work that's actually useful to a practitioner in the field, not just an academic exercise."
